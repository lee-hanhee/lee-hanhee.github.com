Course Notes

BME205

BME205: Fundamentals of Biomedical Engineering provided a comprehensive introduction to integrating engineering and biological approaches for solving medical and technological problems. The course emphasized the connection between organ-level functions and cellular mechanisms through the study of biological system design principles, medical devices, anatomy, and physiology. Practical laboratory sessions, utilizing Arduinos and MATLAB, offered hands-on experience in measuring physiological signals such as PPG, EDA, EMG, and ECG, bridging theoretical concepts with real-world medical technologies.

ECE259

ECE259: Electromagnetism provided a comprehensive understanding of the fundamental laws of electromagnetics, including Coulomb's law, Gauss' law, Poisson's and Laplace's equations, and Maxwell's equations. Through rigorous application of vector calculus, we explored the relationships between electric and magnetic fields and their sources, delving into field-matter interactions and the electromagnetic perspectives of circuit elements. This course equipped us with the analytical skills necessary to solve complex boundary-value problems and understand the principles of transmission lines.

ECE286

ECE286: Probability and Statistics provided a comprehensive foundation in probability and statistics, essential for engineering applications. The course covered fundamental concepts such as sample spaces, probability definitions, Bayes' theorem, and various probability distributions. Through rigorous exploration of statistical methods, including hypothesis testing, regression analysis, and Bayesian statistics, we developed the skills to analyze and interpret data effectively in engineering contexts.

ESC204

ESC204: Praxis III served as the capstone course in the Engineering Science Foundation Design sequence, challenging students to apply engineering design, communication, and teamwork skills to create a functioning product prototype. My project focused on developing an adaptive lighting system for urban residential buildings in Lagos, Nigeria, optimizing energy usage by monitoring environmental conditions to enhance daily operations while reducing energy consumption for both monetary and environmental benefits. The course culminated in a public showcase, where we presented and demonstrated our innovative design to stakeholders and the general public.

PHY294

PHY294: Quantum and Thermal Physics provided an in-depth exploration of both quantum mechanics and classical statistical mechanics. The course covered advanced topics such as Schrödinger's wave mechanics, tunneling, and atomic spectra, as well as Boltzmann's interpretation of entropy and blackbody radiation. Laboratory work included experiments on thermal diffusivity and the Franck-Hertz experiment, offering practical insights into the theoretical principles discussed in lectures.

AER210

AER210: Vector Calculus and Fluid Mechanics provided a rigorous foundation in multiple integrals, vector calculus, and the principles of continuum fluid mechanics. The course covered topics such as double and triple integrals, Green's theorem, and the divergence and Stokes's theorems, alongside the basic conservation laws in fluid mechanics. Hands-on laboratory experiments in flow visualization and microfluidics complemented the theoretical lectures, enhancing both practical understanding and technical report writing skills.

View Notes

CHE260

CHE260: Thermodyanmics and Heat Transfer provided a comprehensive study of classical thermodynamics and its engineering applications, focusing on energy, heat, work, and entropy. The course covered the first and second laws of thermodynamics, properties of substances and mixtures, phase equilibrium, and mechanisms of heat transfer, including conduction, convection, and radiation. Laboratory work included experiments on the Ideal Gas Law and the first law of thermodynamics, reinforcing theoretical concepts through practical application and enhancing analytical skills.

View Notes

ECE253

ECE253: Digital and Computer Systems offered an in-depth study of digital system design principles, focusing on logic circuits, finite state machines, and programmable logic devices. The course included extensive work with Verilog hardware description language and covered essential topics such as computer structure, machine language, and memory hierarchy. Laboratory exercises emphasized the design of logic circuits and microprocessor systems using modern computer-aided design tools and FPGA technology, providing hands-on experience in applying theoretical knowledge to practical design challenges.

View Notes

ESC203

ESC203: Engineering and Society explored the intricate relationship between engineering and society from a humanities and social sciences perspective, emphasizing ethics, equity, and the social impacts of technology. The course fostered critical thinking and communication skills through active learning activities, discussion seminars, and formal debates. My final research paper on the ethics of nanotechnology in sunscreen exemplified the application of these principles, demonstrating an ability to analyze and evaluate the sociotechnical, political, and ethical dimensions of emerging technologies.

The course also delved into Fourier series, Laplace transforms, and an introduction to partial differential equations, emphasizing the interpretation of mathematical problems. Practical MATLAB labs reinforced these concepts, enhancing computational skills and the application of theoretical knowledge through numerical methods.

ESC102

Praxis II develops the models and tools of design, communication, teamwork, and professionalism introduced in Praxis I. The course also introduces additional complementary considerations including ethics and equity. In Praxis II students work primarily in small teams to develop and refine their knowledge and skills in through a combination of active lectures, structured interactive studios, and hands-on practical sessions. The design projects in Praxis II are scoped to communities within the broader City of Toronto. Student teams are responsible for identifying and engaging with these communities, and for first framing and then resolving a collaboratively identified opportunity. Praxis II culminates in a public showcase where teams present and demonstrate their designs to their stakeholders and to the general public. Praxis II also continues to support students as they integrate more fully into the Engineering Science learning community. All courses within the Foundation Design sequence use engineering design to provide a context in which students integrate their knowledge, develop their emerging engineering identity, and codify their individual approach to engineering practice.

MSE160

This course will cover both the fundamentals and applications of molecular chemistry as it relates to the properties of materials. Fundamental topics will include: (1) the design of chemical structures and their relationship to optical and electronic properties; (2) the chemistry and physics of covalent and non-covalent bonding; (3) the relationship of atomic bonding to molecular geometry and local symmetry; (4) crystal structures of extended solids; and (5) extension of these principles to electronic structure, elasticity, and vector and tensor descriptions of materials properties. Applications to diverse areas of engineering will be discussed.

In Praxis I students work both individually and in small teams to develop their knowledge and skills in through a combination of active lectures, structured interactive studios, and hands-on practical sessions. PHY180

Mechanics forms the basic background for the understanding of physics. This course on Classical, or Newtonian mechanics, considers the interactions which influence motion. These interactions are described in terms of the concepts of force, momentum and energy. Initially the focus is on the mechanics of a single particle, considering its motion in a particular frame of reference, and transformations between reference frames. Then the dynamics of systems of particles is examined.
